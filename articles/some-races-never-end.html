Some Races Never End — Only the Baton Changes Hands

Mustafa Tuncer — November 2024 — 3 min read

I discovered Kathrine Switzer through Özgür Karcıoğlu's LinkedIn article. In 1967's Boston Marathon, an official tried to rip off her number because women were "too fragile" for marathons. She resisted, finished the race, got disqualified — then ran persistently until women were officially accepted in 1972.

Kathrine Switzer
Boston Marathon, 1967
First woman to officially run the Boston Marathon with a race number. Faced physical resistance mid-race. Ran until women were accepted in 1972.
Marie Skłodowska-Curie
1891 – 1934
Only woman studying science at Sorbonne in 1891. Initially excluded from her 1903 Nobel. Rejected by the French Academy even after her second Nobel in 1911.

Marie Skłodowska-Curie faced similar barriers. The only woman studying science at Sorbonne in 1891, initially excluded from her 1903 Nobel (added only through her husband's insistence), rejected by the French Academy even after her second Nobel in 1911.

She kept running: two Nobels, radium and polonium discoveries, wartime X-ray units.

Because they ran, Alara doesn't need permission to run.

Today, my daughter Alara Tuncer pursues her doctorate at University of Toronto Sunnybrook, walking the path they opened. But she still runs every day.
